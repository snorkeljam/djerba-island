Homer · ~800 BC

The Island
That Makes You Forget

"Whoever ate the honey-sweet fruit of the lotus had no desire to come back. All they now wanted was to stay here, eating lotus, forgetting the way home."
— Homer, The Odyssey

Homer wrote it 2,800 years ago. Odysseus and his crew, storm-battered and homesick, stumbled onto a beautiful island. The locals offered them fruit. Three sailors ate. And then, without violence, without chains, without any threat — they simply stopped wanting to leave.

Odysseus had to drag his own men back to the ships. They wept like children being pulled away from something wonderful. They had forgotten who they were.

Scholars believe that island was Djerba.

It's a flat, sun-drugged place covered in olive trees and date palms, ringed by shallow turquoise water. Beautiful in the way that traps are beautiful. Close enough to the mainland to be reached, remote enough to feel escaped from. Its name has changed a dozen times across 6,000 years: Meninx to the Romans, Lytos to the Greeks. The island doesn't mind what you call it. It has outlasted every name.

Every empire that rose in the Mediterranean eventually sent ships here. The Phoenicians. The Romans. The Arabs. The Normans. The Ottomans. The Spanish. The French. They all came. Some left. Some never really left at all.

"Whoever ate the honey-sweet fruit of the lotus had no desire to come back and bring us news."
The lotus wasn't actually a flower
Botanists think Homer's "lotus" was the fruit of the jujube tree — a small, sweet, date-like berry that grows across North Africa. Ancient sailors called it lotus. Eat enough of it, combined with heat, exhaustion, and an island that gives you no reason to keep suffering — maybe you really would forget where you were supposed to be going.
Phoenicians · 1200–800 BC

The Color of
Rot and Royalty

The Phoenicians came to Djerba to make the most expensive color in the world. They crushed thousands of sea snails to do it. The island smelled like death and sold its product for more than gold.

The PhoeniciansAncient seafaring traders from the eastern Mediterranean — modern-day Lebanon. Master navigators and merchants who founded colonies across North Africa and Spain from around 1200 BC. were the great sailors of the ancient world, and around 1200 BC they chose Djerba. What they found here was murexA spiky, fist-sized sea snail found in Mediterranean shallows. Each one contains one tiny gland with a few drops of purple fluid. You need roughly 10,000 snails to make a single gram of dye. — a spiky sea snail in the coastal shallows. Inside each snail was a tiny gland holding a fluid that, when exposed to sunlight, turned a deep, unfading purple. Tyrian purpleThe most valuable dye in the ancient world, made only from crushed murex snails. It took its name from Tyre, the great Phoenician city. The color was so rare it became the symbol of emperors, kings, and gods — and smelled like rotting fish while being made.. The color of emperors and gods.

To harvest it, you crushed thousands of snails at once. The flesh rotted. The fluid ran. The smell was so vile that ancient cities kept their dye factories on the outskirts, downwind. Djerba's purple was said to rival even the legendary purple of Tyre.

A small flat island in the sea, reeking of rot, producing a fluid worth more per ounce than gold, worn only by the most powerful people in the world. The people who lived there couldn't afford to wear it. They just made it, and ships came and paid, and the cycle continued for centuries.

The Amazigh were here before all of them
Before the Phoenicians, before anyone who left written records, the Amazigh — the Berber people — were already here. They arrived around 1200 BC as part of the sea-peoples migrations that shook the entire ancient Mediterranean. Through every conquest, every religion, every new flag — the Amazigh stayed. You can find their descendants on Djerba today, still speaking a version of a language older than Rome.
Roman Empire · 146 BC

The Road They Built
to Control You

When Rome destroyed Carthage in 146 BC, they inherited everything — including Djerba. They didn't trust an island they couldn't reach by land. So they built a road straight to it across the sea. That road is still there.

The Romans were engineers of control. An island that ships could reach but land armies couldn't was full of dangerous possibilities — smugglers, rebels, people who could hide things. So they built the El Kantara causewaySix kilometers of stone road built across the shallow sea between Djerba and the North African mainland. "El Kantara" means "the bridge" in Arabic. Built by Rome around 100 BC, it still carries cars today under a modern road laid over it in 1951.: six kilometers of stone road across shallow sea, connecting Djerba to the North African mainland.

Think about what that meant. One road in, one road out. The Romans could tax every cart that crossed it, count every traveler, seal the island in an afternoon. The causeway that was supposed to connect Djerba to the world was really designed to keep it under watch.

Their capital on the island was called Meninx — a trade crossroads linking Africa, the East, and Europe. Slaves, dye, grain, oil. All of it moved through Djerba.

The causeway the Romans built two thousand years ago is still in use. They paved a modern road over it in 1951. When you drive to Djerba today, you drive on Roman stone.

Rome named the island "colored cloth"
Older sources called it by a name that translates roughly to "the island of cloths" — a reference to the purple dye trade still running when Rome took over. The Phoenicians had built the entire economy around color. Rome just took over the business and added taxes.
586 BC – Present

The Synagogue
Older Than Christ

In the middle of a Muslim island, there is a Jewish synagogue that may be 2,600 years old. The two communities lived side by side for nearly two millennia. Then the attacks came — not from neighbors, but from the darkest edges of the world.

In 586 BC, the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II destroyed Solomon's Temple in Jerusalem and drove the Jewish people into exile. According to legend, a group of priests carried a sacred stone from the Temple's ruins all the way to a small island in North Africa. They built a shelter for it. They called it El Ghriba — "the miraculous."

That shelter became a synagogue. That synagogue still stands today.

El Ghriba may be the oldest synagogue in the world. It predates Christianity by six centuries. Its community survived the Phoenicians, the Romans, the Arab conquest, the Ottoman Empire, the French colonial period, and two world wars. At its peak, Djerba had around 4,500 Jewish residents — one of the most ancient Jewish communities in the Arab world. For nearly two thousand years, the Jewish and Muslim communities of Djerba lived together in a coexistence rare in any era or any place.

Then the attacks came — three times in four decades, each one carried out by a lone individual driven by extremist ideology, condemned each time by the island's people.

1985
A gunman opened fire into the synagogue compound. Five people killed, four of them Jewish worshippers at prayer.
2002
A man linked to Al-Qaeda detonated a truck bomb outside El Ghriba during Passover. Twenty-one people killed. The act was condemned worldwide.
2023
An armed attacker opened fire during the annual El Ghriba pilgrimage. Five killed. Another officer stopped the attack. Mourners returned the following year.

Every time, the community buried its dead. Every time, they came back. There are roughly 1,100 Jewish residents on Djerba today. They have been there, unbroken, for 2,600 years. The annual pilgrimage to El Ghriba still draws thousands of visitors from around the world — and the island receives them.

The Temple stone under the floor
The legend says the sacred stone carried from Solomon's Temple is still somewhere beneath El Ghriba's floor. No archaeologist has ever been given permission to look for it. The community prefers the mystery to the proof. Some things are more powerful when they remain uncertain.
Turgut Reis · 1514–1565

The Pirate Who
Owned the Sea

His name was Dragut. He went to sea at age twelve. By forty he was the most terrifying man in the Mediterranean. The Ottoman Empire made him Governor of Djerba, and he used it as his home port to raid, burn, and enslave for twenty years.

Turgut Reis — "Dragut" to Europeans — was born in Anatolia around 1514. He started as a sailor, became a soldier, became a corsairA corsair was a kind of state-sponsored pirate. Unlike common pirates who worked for themselves, corsairs operated under letters of permission from a government — in this case the Ottoman Empire. They split their profits with the state and were used as a kind of unofficial navy.. The corsairs raided Christian ships and coastal towns under semi-official Ottoman sanction across the entire Mediterranean.

Dragut was the best of them. He was called "the Drawn Sword of Islam" and "the Greatest Pirate Warrior of All Time." In 1539, the Ottomans made him Governor of Djerba. For the next twenty years, the island was his headquarters, his home port, his fortress.

From Djerba, he organized raids on the Italian coast — burning villages, killing men, taking women and children as slaves. The Barbary corsairs raided as far north as Britain, Ireland, and Iceland. An estimated one million Europeans were enslaved by Barbary corsairs between 1500 and 1800.

Dragut was captured himself in 1540 — taken as a galleyA long, low warship propelled primarily by oars — typically rowed by slaves or condemned criminals chained to their benches. Galley slaves worked until they died or were ransomed. Mediterranean sea battles in this era were largely fought between fleets of galleys. slave by the Genoese admiral Andrea Doria. He spent two years chained to an oar before another corsair ransomed him. He came back angrier than before.

The year he was a slave himself
In 1540, the same man who had captured and enslaved thousands was himself chained to a galley oar. He spent two years rowing for his enemies. The story goes that when the corsair admiral Barbarossa came to negotiate his release, the Genoese captor said Dragut wasn't for sale at any price — so Barbarossa played him at chess, and won him back. Whether that story is exactly true or not, it is the kind of story that follows men like Dragut.
May 11, 1560

The Tower
of Skulls

On a beach on Djerba, the Ottomans built a tower from the bones of dead Spanish soldiers. Twenty-five feet high. Sixty feet around. It stood for three hundred years.

In 1559, King Philip II of Spain assembled the largest Christian fleet in a generation. The plan: retake Tripoli. The fleet numbered over fifty ships — Spanish, Papal, Genoese, Maltese, Neapolitan. They stopped at Djerba to resupply.

They never left.

On the morning of May 11, 1560, an Ottoman fleet of eighty-six galleys arrived under Admiral Piyale Pasha — with Dragut alongside him. The Christian fleet was anchored and unprepared. The battle lasted a few hours. Twenty-seven Christian galleys were captured or sunk. The rest scattered.

What followed was a three-month siege of the Spanish fort. When it fell, the Spanish commander Don Álvaro de Sande was taken prisoner, along with roughly five thousand soldiers, hauled in chains to Istanbul and paraded through the city as trophies.

Then the Ottomans built something.

From the bodies of the Spanish dead, they constructed Borj el-Jemajem"Borj el-Jemajem" means Citadel of Skulls in Arabic. Built in 1560 from the bones of defeated Spanish soldiers on the beach at Djerba. It stood for 288 years as a monument to Ottoman power, visible to every ship passing through the straits. — the Citadel of Skulls. A pyramid of human skulls and bones, twenty-five feet high and sixty feet around, on a public beach. Not hidden. Not buried. On a beach. Where every ship passing could see it. Where every traveler walked past it.

It stood until 1848 — nearly three hundred years — before local Christians obtained permission to dismantle it and give the remains a proper burial.

For three hundred years, a pile of human skulls stood on a beach. Children played near it. Fishermen walked past it every morning. Ships' captains stared at it from the water.

What happened to the Spanish commander
Don Álvaro de Sande survived. He was ransomed, returned to Spain, and continued his military career. He died in 1573 as Governor of Milan — old, decorated, and haunted by Djerba. He wrote accounts of the battle but rarely spoke of the three months he spent watching his men die, then watching their skulls get stacked into a monument.

Present Day

The Island
That Won't Die

The Vandals came. The Byzantines. The Arabs. The Normans. The Ottomans. The French. Each one thought they had finally settled the question of who Djerba belongs to. None of them were right.

Djerba today is a tourist destination. Hotels line the beaches. People come from Europe for the sun. On the surface it looks like any Mediterranean island where the past has been smoothed down and varnished over for visitors.

But the past refuses to behave here.

The Jewish community — 2,600 years old, the descendants of people who fled Babylon — is still here, still being targeted. The 2002 truck bomb at El Ghriba killed twenty-one people. The 2023 attack killed five. Between those events, thousands of pilgrims still come from around the world for the annual El Ghriba festival, lighting candles in a building that has survived longer than most countries. The island receives them.

The Amazigh Berber people, who arrived before the Phoenicians, are still here. Their language is still spoken. Their culture practiced. They were here first, and every invader underestimated how quietly a people can survive.

The El Kantara causeway, built by Romans two thousand years ago, still carries cars every day. El Ghriba synagogue, built to house a stone carried from a burning temple in 586 BC, still holds services on Friday nights. The harbor where Dragut loaded his stolen cargo is now a marina for pleasure boats.

Where the Tower of Skulls once stood, there is a small commemorative plaque. The beach is clean. Children swim there now.

The island doesn't care. It has seen worse. It will see more.

The island's name might mean "the miracle"
One theory holds that the word "Djerba" shares a root with "El Ghriba" — meaning "the miraculous" or "the strange." An island named after the quality of surviving impossible things. Whether that etymology is exactly right or not, it fits.
